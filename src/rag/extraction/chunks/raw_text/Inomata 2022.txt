Abstract  
In this paper , we present new referential statistics for supply chain exposure to 
concentration risk of global production networks.  The studyâ€™s net contribution rests on 
the development of a metric that indicates exposure risk in terms of the frequency  of 
supply chain engagement with the regions of analytical concern, alongside the 
traditional approach based on volume measures of valu e-added concentration.  
Japan, a country with a high propensity to encounter natural hazards, and China, 
under mounting geopolitical tension with the United States, are chosen as the target 
regions  for risk assessment. In addition, the highly asymmetric structure of mutual 
economic dependency in the US -China relations is identified . 
 
Keywords :  
  risk assessment, global supply chains, supply chain exposure, network concentration, 
choke points, input -output analysis

1. Introduction  
The world  economy in the 21st century has given rise to a new production 
arrangement known as global value chains  (GVCs) , in which production processes are 
sliced and relocated to places where the corresponding tasks are most efficiently 
performed.  Due to  the rapid advancement of transportation modes and information and 
communication technology  (ICT) , production networks continue to expand to cover every 
corner of the globe . Concurrently, the pursuit of opti mal allocation of resources often 
results in the agglomeration and concentration of key production capacities in a certain 
region of a certain country.  
This may work well in  an ordinary environment , but when circumstances 
change  for the worse , such production hubs can become  â€œchoke pointsâ€ of the entire 
economic system. Multiple examples can be found in recent history: the Lehman Shock, 
the Great  East Japan Earthquake, and various forms of cyber -attacks, where hyper  
economic interdependency rendered production and financial sys tems particularly 
vulnerable to a single point of failure.   
This series of incidents has driven our attention to the systematic risk 
associated with the skewed  concentration of supply chains in global production networks.

 The flow of goods, money, people, and information jointly form a highly compl ex nexus of 
economic activities, and a shock generated in a core  region may rapidly and extensively 
propagate to other regions  across national borders in an unforeseeable manner.  
Against this backdrop, we propose a novel approach to construct a risk indicator 
for firmsâ€™ business operations, especially in a global context, by mapping the degree of 
network  concentration of supply c hains. This is done along a line of traditional 
techniques in input -output economics known as the â€œkey sector analysis,â€ yet with 
methodological augmentation by a compatible analytical framework in network theory.  
The novelty of this study is that we meas ure concentration risks in terms of the 
frequency  with which a particular supply chain passes through a high -risk region, as 
opposed to the  conventional approach  based on a volume  concept . If the analysis is 
directed to supply chain disruptions (such as na tural disasters or geopolitical conflicts), 
then the measurement will reveal the degree of supply chain vulnerability to 
unpredictable incidents in the region of analytical concern.

The HEM was first introduced into the int ernational context by Dietzenbacher

Recent applications of HEM  to internati onal trade are found in the studies of

Another  important strand of HEM applications is in the field of environmental

From the perspective of risk analysis, there are volume  and frequency 
dimensions  of risk assessment. For example, the chances that our families get infected  
with  the virus will be high, either because they visit a risky place  together, or because a 
family member , even if just one,  visits such a place  frequently. Alternatively, perhaps 
even a more straightforward analogy is that event s of earthquakes are reported and 
analyzed with respect to both the magnitude and frequency of occurrence.  
Paraphrasing it to fit  our context, a supply chain is considered highly exposed 
to a specific country risk  if its product contains a significant volume of value -added 
sourced from the country in question , or if the production activities along the supply 
chain involve frequent engagement with  the countryâ€™s industrial sectors . The first factor  
is self -evident , but the second factor  is also important because it relates to the probability  
aspect of the supply chain being  caught by contingencies in the country of concern . The 
traditional key secto r analys is, however, can only address the  volume -wise scale of inter -
sectoral  spillovers , be it backward/forward linkages or the HEM . It is, therefore, 
impossible  to capture in isolation these dual attributes  â€• volume  and frequency  â€• of 
concentration risks .

 6. Conclusion  
In this paper , we present ed and analyze d new referential statistics for the risk 
assessment of the network  concentration of global supply chains. The studyâ€™s net 
contribution rests on the development of a metric that  indicates concentration in terms 
of the frequency  of supply chain engagement with the regions of analytical concern, 
alongside the traditional approach based on volume measures of value -added 
concentration.   
Japan, a country with a high  propensity to encounter natural hazards , and China, 
under mounting geopolitical tension s with the U nited States , were chosen as the target 
regions fo r risk assessment. The analysis followed a line of techniques in input -output 
economics known as the â€œkey sector analysis,â€ yet with methodological augmentation 
employing  a compatible analytical framework in network theory. Using the latest set of 
multi -country input -output tables constructed by the OECD, the following findings  were  
presented . 
The supply chains of the selected industrialized economies are generally more 
concentrated in China than in Japan. The study of China as a target region  reveals that 
the nature of concentration risks differs between advanced economies and emerging 
economies, such that the former is more prone to facing frequency risks than volume

36 
 risks, while the opposite is observed for the latter. For Japan as a target region, 
Taiwanese industr ies stand out for being dependent on the country, especially with 
regard to the share of value -added origins.  
Focusing on the ICT equipment industry, the supply chains of Korea and 
Chinese Taipei are most exposed to concentration r isks in China, both in terms of volume 
and frequency. In contrast, the US supply chains present an interesting case ; the low 
concentration of its value -added origin in China may be a straightforward reflection of 
the US economyâ€™s sheer size, while its frequent exposure to Chinaâ€™s geographic territory  
is associated with the likelihood of being caught by contingencies in the country.  
In general, the two metrics of network concentration in the volume and 
frequency terms  were positively correlated. However, th e above US case suggests that 
only considering the volume side may lead to a significant underestimation of the overall 
risk in supply chain management.  
Finally , the analysis of mutual risk positions for the US and Chinaâ€™s supply 
chains reveal ed a notable asymmetry in the dependence structure between the two 
countries, posing a significant concern to US â€™ strategic autonomy from China.

 Appendix D    Multi -country input -output tables  
 
A multi -country input -output table brings together national tables of different 
countries into a single framework and thus has the same basic structure as a national 
input -output table. The rows of the table show the supply sectors of products, the 
columns  represent demand sectors for products, and the intersection of a row and a 
column indicates the value of transaction exchanged between these two sectors. However, 
the distinctive feature of multi -country tables is that they explicitly present 
internationa l transactions in the form of import/export matrices by trading partners, 
which allows for a comprehensive mapping of global production networks.
